## The Bogleheads Investing Philosophy and How to "Stay the Course"

When I started by first job out of college at a startup, the CFO asked me what I wanted to do for 401k contributions. I told him to just max it out. He said “Good answer.” But I didn’t know anything about investing, and just picked some fund without much thought. It wasn’t until seven years later that I started to look into what I should actually do for investments.

I found [The Bogleheads' Guide to Investing](https://www.amazon.com/Bogleheads-Guide-Investing-Taylor-Larimore/dp/0470067365). It really resonated, and kicked off my interest in personal finance. That book gave me a framework for both what to invest in and how to think about risk. 

## Jack Bogle & Bogleheads

Jack Bogle (1929–2019) was the [founder of Vanguard](https://www.bogleheads.org/wiki/Getting_started). He invented the index fund as a way to lower the costs of investing for average people. His invention changed the investing world in a fundamental way.

Before index funds, investing was about picking a mutual fund and betting that it would outperform the market. Bogle looked at the data and realized that almost no funds beat the market after even 5–10 years, especially after accounting for fees. Instead of trying to beat the market, index funds own the whole market or a very large representative subset.

They don’t get above average returns, they get exactly the average. But that turns out to be great, and the cost is very low because you're not paying someones salary, and also because they minimize trades and associated fees inside the fund. 

Bogleheads is an online community that focuses on investing advice inspired by John Bogle. There are forums with hundreds of thousands of posts going back decades. They have a [specific philosophy](https://www.bogleheads.org/wiki/Bogleheads%C2%AE_investment_philosophy). I’ll try to distill the most important parts here.

## Invest early and often

Live below your means. You need to have money left over every month so that you have something to invest. Start investing as early as you can, because the amount of time your money has to grow is one of the top factors that determines the ultimate value of your investments.

The second most important factor is how much you invest. Investing more beats most other types of optimizations, including what you invest in. Both factors are inputs in how compound interest increases iver time. 

> "Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. He who understands it, earns it; he who doesn’t, pays it."
> — Albert Einstein

What you invest in is relatively unimportant. Make it simple, such as a set amount every month. Make it automatic, ideally every paycheck. 401k plans make this easy with contributions directly from payroll. If you have funds beyond that, making automatic contributions into taxable accounts is the next best way to increase how much you invest. 

Side note: I was wary for way too long about setting up a brokerage account to make investments above and beyond my 401k. This is one of my largest regrets about investing, not investing more than the 401k max sooner.

## Use index funds

Index funds are known as “passive,” in that they don’t require a person make decions on what they invest in or when they buy and sell. That’s opposed to “active,” where there is a person or board, and you are paying them a fee to manage the fund. 

Passive beats active partly because active managers charge 1% or more for this service. This comes right off the top of your investment returns. So if a fund earned 7% growth in a year, you actually get 6%. That’s 14% of your return gone, even though managers make it sound like it’s “just” 1%. Thanks to compounding, it adds up to a lot over 20 years or more. 

But the bigger reason index funds win is that even professional managers can’t beat the market very consistently.

![Active Managers vs Index](/blog/images/spiva-after-tax-scorecard-year-end-2024-exhibit-2.png)

From [SPIVA After-Tax Scorecard Year-End 2024: The Effect of Taxes on Indices and Active Funds](https://www.spglobal.com/spdji/en/spiva/article/spiva-after-tax-scorecard)

Today, this is a well understood fact acknowledged by the industry. 

> “Most investors, both institutional and individual, will find that the best way to own common stocks is through an index fund that charges minimal fees. Those following this path are sure to beat the net results (after fees and expenses) delivered by the great majority of investment professionals.”  
> — Warren Buffett ([1996 letter to shareholders](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/1996ar/1996.html))

Finally, index funds are naturally diversified investments, which is key to reducing risk and volatility.

## Stay the course

Don’t try to time the market. That means don’t hold off investing just because the market is at a high, and don’t sell just because the market is low. No one actually knows whether the market is high or low, because no one knows what the market will do tomorrow or a year from now.

Missing just a few of the market’s best days can drastically impact your returns.

> “If you’re out of the market on the best days, your long-term returns can be significantly reduced.”  
> — J.P. Morgan Asset Management

![img](/blog/images/timing-market-best-days.jpg)

From [Timing the Market: Why It’s So Hard, in One Chart](https://www.visualcapitalist.com/chart-timing-the-market/)

Staying the course means not selling if the market crashes. The market will crash, it’s just a matter of when.

## How to Stay the Course

Here in early 2026, there is a fear that the market will crash at some point due to an AI bubble, perhaps exacerbated by a large concentration in the market from a small number of companies. If you subscribe to the tenets above, what should you do?

## This time is **not** different

First, you need to realize that this is not unusual. Market crashes are a fact of investing and economies. There will be a crash, it’s just a matter of when, and there will be another one after that.

> “The four most dangerous words in investing are: ‘this time it’s different.’”  
> — Sir John Templeton

In the moment, people often feel like a particular crash is not recoverable. That there is a special reason why this crash will not behave like all the others before it.

* 1929 – Crash & Great Depression: The market collapsed and was immediately followed by cascading bank failures, a collapse in industrial production, and mass unemployment. It felt like the financial system itself might be breaking.
* 1962 – Cuban Missile Crisis: Markets were overshadowed by the U.S. and Soviet Union approaching nuclear conflict. The fear was that geopolitical escalation could permanently alter or destroy the global economic system.
* 1987 – Black Monday: The Dow dropped 22.6% in a single day, the largest one-day decline in history. The speed and scale of the crash created raised doubts about whether stability could be restored.
* 2008–2010 – Global Financial Crisis: The collapse of major financial institutions and the freezing of credit markets exposed deep structural weaknesses in the global financial system. Again, it felt like the whole system was failing. 
* 2020 – COVID-19 Pandemic Crash: Global markets plunged as governments shut down economies and imposed stay-at-home orders. Economic activity was abruptly halted across industries, with no clear timeline or mechanism for recovery.￼

In all these cases, many people pulled their money out of the stock market, even after losing a lot of value. But the markets did recover, and they have always recovered, to date.

## What to do ahead of time

If you assume that the next crash is coming at some point, what do you do?

Write down your own personal [Investment Policy Statement (IPS)](https://www.bogleheads.org/wiki/Investment_policy_statement), which is your plan for how to invest and what to do in the face of volatility. It should include an asset allocation (AA), which represents the split of stocks and bonds in your portfolio.

The IPS should also include your plan to rebalance your AA, regardless of whether you’re in a crash.

You can also build a cash emergency fund. In the case of a crash, you will not be forced to sell at a loss until these funds are spent down. During your working years, this mainly covers the risk of losing your job as part of a crash. A good rule of thumb is 6–12 months of expenses.

If you truly think the world is about to end, you can’t protect yourself.

> “When Timur sacked Aleppo and Damascus in 1400, it didn’t help to have your savings in gold. You lost your life and your gold.”
> — Marc Faber

## When a crash happens, what should you do?

The most important thing is to stay invested. Do not sell. Selling is how you lock in losses and never recover.

Your friends and neighbors will be telling you to sell.

Instead, keep buying if you have a job. Assets are effectively cheaper during downturns.

> “The time to buy is when there’s blood in the streets.”  
> — John D. Rockefeller

Rebalance as needed based on your IPS. This is a version of buying low and selling high.

Reduce expenses. Stop checking the news and the market. If this made you nervous, consider adjusting your AA after the crash.

## FAQ

### Why settle for average?

You’re not settling, you’re sidestepping a losing game. The market is very hard to beat. Market returns are good enough to build serious wealth over time, and they are reliable and repeatable.

### Why do you think you don’t need a professional?

Most people don’t need a professional *to pick investments*. A diversified portfolio of low-cost index funds aligned to your risk tolerance is most of the battle. You may still need a professional for other things like estate planning.

### Why doesn’t everyone do this?

Because it’s simple, but it’s not easy. It’s boring. It feels like leaving money on the table. Staying the course during a crash is hard.

### What if this time is different?

Maybe it is. You just can’t reliably act on that. If something truly is different, it gets priced in quickly. By the time you’re acting on it, you’re usually late.

To benefit from “this time is different,” you have to be right about when to get out of the market and when to get back in.

### If you stay invested in index funds through a crash, do you personally fully recover value?

If you stay invested, your portfolio value recovers and goes on to new highs. This is especially true of index funds, because they capture the total market recovery. The timing can take years.

### What if many specific companies in the index go out of business?

Every year, companies in the index go bankrupt or fade. The index automatically replaces the losers with stronger or growing companies. Indexing ensures you own the big winners when they emerge.

## Conclusion

The Bogleheads philosophy is simple, but it requires discipline. Invest early and often, use low-cost index funds, and stay the course through market volatility. Most of the advantage comes not from clever strategies, but from consistency and avoiding mistakes.

If you follow this approach, you are not trying to outsmart the market. You are accepting how it works and positioning yourself to benefit from it over decades. That shift in mindset is what makes the strategy durable.
